(3) Outline of 2-Cycle Engine Operation

Engines are classified into 2-cycle and 4-cycle engines, and their operating states differ significantly. In a 2-cycle engine, the operations of compression and expansion are repeated. Fuel explosion and expansion occur every time the piston makes one reciprocation, and power is transmitted as the crankshaft completes one rotation per power stroke. As shown in Figure 2-5, the structure does not have intake or exhaust valves. Instead, it has intake and exhaust ports at the lower part of the cylinder (near the bottom dead center of the piston stroke). The piston acts as a valve to perform intake and exhaust operations. The outline of the operation is as follows:

(A) When the cylinder compression (piston upward stroke) is completed, fuel is injected and ignited simultaneously, causing combustion and pushing the piston down. (Combustion stroke or power stroke)

(B) As the piston descends, the exhaust port on the lower side of the cylinder opens, and the combustion gas escapes.

(C) As the piston further descends due to the rotation of the crank, it opens the intake port, and the air sent by a separate pump enters the cylinder. This completely discharges the remaining exhaust gas (scavenging), and the compression of air begins in the next upward stroke. In this way, the 2-cycle engine has two explosion strokes compared to the 4-cycle engine. Theoretically, it should produce twice the output, but in reality, it is about 1.5 times.
