(3) 2-Stroke Engine Operation Overview
Engines include 2-stroke and 4-stroke engines, and their operating states differ greatly. In other words, in a 2-stroke engine, the two strokes of compression and expansion are repeated, and the fuel explodes and expands every time the piston makes one reciprocation. The crank shaft transmits power with each rotation being a power stroke. As shown in Figure 2-5, the structure does not have intake or exhaust valves. Intake and exhaust ports are provided in the lower part of the cylinder (near the bottom dead center of the piston stroke), and the piston also acts as a valve, performing intake and exhaust operations. The operation overview is as follows:

(A) When cylinder compression (piston upward stroke) is completed, fuel is injected and ignited simultaneously, so combustion occurs and the piston is pushed down (combustion stroke or power stroke).

(B) When the piston descends, the exhaust port on the lower side of the cylinder opens and the combustion gas escapes.

(C) When the piston descends further due to the rotation of the crank, it opens the intake port, and the air sent by a separate pump enters the cylinder, completely discharging the remaining exhaust gas, and compression of the air begins in the next upward stroke. In this way, since the explosion stroke of a 2-stroke engine is performed twice compared to a 4-stroke engine, the output should theoretically be twice as high, but in reality, it is said to be about 1.5 times.
