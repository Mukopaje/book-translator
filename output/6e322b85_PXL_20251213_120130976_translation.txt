(C) Combustion Stroke (See Figure 2-3)
With the intake valve S and exhaust valve E closed, the piston (P) moves toward the end of its compression stroke, i.e., just before the crank reaches top dead center and toward the beginning of this stroke.
When pressurized fuel is sprayed into the cylinder by the fuel injector in a mist, it spontaneously ignites upon contact with the hot air. The fuel explodes and burns within the cylinder, and the rapid expansion of the combustion gases pushes the piston down, passing through the connecting rod and generating torque for the crankshaft.
This stroke is called the combustion stroke or power stroke. 1
(D) Exhaust Stroke (See Figure 1, 2-3)
If the exhaust valve E is open, as the piston rises past bottom dead center, the combustion gases are expelled into the atmosphere, and are completely expelled when the piston reaches top dead center.
During the combustion stroke, the cam rotates the crankshaft slightly before the piston (P) reaches bottom dead center.

(Saturday Reception)
Figure 2-4 shows the pressure-displacement (PV) diagram for a four-stroke engine.
When gas is introduced into the cylinder and the piston is gradually compressed from position A to position B, or conversely, when the expanding force of the gas pushes the piston from position B to position A, the pressure is generally represented by P on the vertical axis and the volume by V on the horizontal axis to represent the change in gas state within the cylinder. The relationship between the two (changes in gas volume and pressure within the cylinder) is shown graphically.
When the volume is pushed in, it is V2, and the resulting pressure is represented by P2.
This diagram is called a PV diagram or pressure-displacement diagram.
In other words, if the volume when the piston is at A is V1 and the pressure is Pi, then when the piston is at B, it is called a PV diagram or pressure-displacement diagram.
