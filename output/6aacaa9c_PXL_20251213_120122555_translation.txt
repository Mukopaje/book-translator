(3) Operating Summary of 2-Stroke Engine
Engines include 2-stroke engines and 4-stroke engines, and their operating states differ greatly. In other words, in a 2-stroke engine, the two strokes of compression and expansion are repeated, and fuel explosion and expansion occur every time the piston makes one reciprocating motion, so the power stroke becomes the working stroke for each revolution of the crankshaft, and power is transmitted. As shown in Figure 2-5, the structure does not have intake or exhaust valves, but instead has intake and exhaust ports at the bottom of the cylinder (near the bottom dead center of the piston stroke). The piston also acts as a valve to perform the intake and exhaust functions. The outline of the operation is as follows:

(A) When cylinder compression (piston upstroke) is completed, fuel is injected and ignited at the same time, so combustion occurs and the piston is pushed down. (Combustion stroke or working stroke)

(B) When the piston descends, the exhaust port on the lower side of the cylinder opens and the combustion gas escapes.

(C) When the piston descends further due to the rotation of the crank, it opens the intake port, and the air sent by a separate pump enters the cylinder, completely discharging the remaining exhaust gas and scavenging it, and the compression of air begins in the next upward stroke. In this way, the 2-stroke engine has two explosion strokes compared to the 4-stroke engine, so theoretically it should have twice the output, but in reality it is about 1.5 times.
