(3) Overview of 2-Cycle Engine Operation

Engines are classified into 2-cycle and 4-cycle types, and their operational states differ significantly. Specifically, a 2-cycle engine operates with two strokes: compression and expansion. With each complete reciprocation of the piston, fuel undergoes explosive expansion, and the crankshaft transmits power with each rotation. As illustrated in Figure 2-5, the structure lacks intake and exhaust valves. Instead, intake and exhaust ports are located at the lower part of the cylinder (near the bottom dead center of the piston stroke). The piston itself acts as a valve, performing both intake and exhaust functions. The operational overview is as follows:

(A) When cylinder compression (piston upward stroke) ends, fuel is injected and ignited simultaneously, causing combustion that pushes the piston downward. (Combustion stroke or power stroke)

(B) As the piston descends, the exhaust port on the side of the lower cylinder opens, allowing the combustion gases to escape.

(C) As the piston continues to descend due to the crank rotation, it opens the intake port, allowing air delivered by a separate pump to enter the cylinder. This air expels any remaining exhaust gases and begins compressing the air for the next upward stroke. Thus, compared to a 4-cycle engine, a 2-cycle engine theoretically produces twice the output due to two power strokes per cycle. However, in practice, the output is approximately 1.5 times.

(1) Compression Ratio

The compression ratio indicates the extent to which the air or air-fuel mixture drawn into the cylinder is compressed. It is represented by A, the displacement volume when the piston moves from the bottom dead center to the top dead center. When the piston reaches the top dead center, the compression ratio is determined by the volume of the piston head.
